# Aesthetic Manual

Use this manual to judge and improve scene-content prompts. The goal is not subjective decoration; the goal is stable visual taste based on realism, scene logic, visual hierarchy, material detail, restrained commercial expression, and memory value.

## Core Position

Good scene prompts are not made by stacking adjectives. They are made by choosing specific visual evidence:

- What is actually in the place
- Why each object belongs there
- What the person or product is doing
- How the environment proves the scene is real
- Which detail makes the frame memorable
- How the product is naturally useful inside the scene

## 1. Realism First

The scene should feel like a real moment that could happen in the product's world.

Prefer:

- Work traces, hand marks, dust, condensation, worn labels, opened boxes, scattered tools, used notebooks, imperfect surfaces
- Natural human gestures: checking, adjusting, wiping, waiting, comparing, recording, reacting
- Industry-specific environment details when they are known

Avoid:

- Over-clean showroom spaces when the product belongs in a working environment
- Perfectly staged advertising poses without a reason
- Random luxury surfaces, sci-fi screens, floating UI, particles, smoke, or glowing effects

## 2. Scene Logic

Every detail should have a reason to exist.

Ask:

- Why is the person here?
- Why is the product here?
- What task is happening?
- What happened just before this moment?
- What physical evidence proves the task is real?

Remove details that are only decorative and do not support the scene, product, emotion, or believability.

## 3. Visual Layers

Avoid "one subject in front of one background." Build depth through relationships.

Useful layer types:

- Foreground: tools, hands, documents, leaves, fabric edge, table corner, packaging, cables, sample bags
- Subject area: person, product, key action, active object
- Background: workplace, field, room, shelves, doorway, window, machinery, people at distance, environmental traces

Layers should guide attention without requiring camera-direction language.

## 4. Material, Color, And Light

Use light and material as evidence, not decoration.

Good prompts specify:

- Material: brushed metal, matte plastic, wet soil, frosted glass, cotton fabric, rubber grip, paper label
- Surface behavior: scratches, fingerprints, dust, reflection, translucency, moisture, worn edges
- Light quality: soft window light, cool lab light, low warm practical light, overcast daylight, directional side light
- Shadow behavior: soft contact shadows, long shadows, diffuse shadow, hard equipment shadow
- Color relationship: muted earth tones with clean product contrast, cool gray lab palette with small warning-color accents

Avoid using "高级感" as a substitute for real material and light decisions.

## 5. Restrained Commercial Expression

The product should be visible because it is doing something useful, not because it is placed like a billboard.

Prefer:

- Product in use
- Product causing a visible response
- Product surrounded by relevant task evidence
- A human reaction that shows value without exaggeration
- Screen, indicator, texture, or result shown only when it is relevant and not factually invented

Avoid:

- Empty hero placement
- Overly polished product glamour shots in unsuitable contexts
- Fake enthusiasm
- Claims that the user did not provide

## 6. Memory Point

Each refined scene should contain one memorable visual point.

Possible memory points:

- A precise human action
- A product detail under meaningful light
- A contrast between messy reality and clean product feedback
- A quiet emotional reaction
- A distinctive industry-specific object
- A material contrast, such as wet soil against a clean sensor surface

Do not add multiple competing memory points. One strong point is usually enough.

## Anti-Cliche Rules

Do not use these unless the user explicitly asks and they fit the scene:

- Generic "cinematic" wording as a quality shortcut
- "8K", "ultra realistic", "masterpiece", or keyword spam
- Random slow motion
- Backlit silhouettes without narrative purpose
- Floating holographic UI
- Excessive particles, fog, bokeh, lens flare, sparks, or dust clouds
- Unmotivated sunset, night city, luxury studio, or sci-fi lab
- Overly clean and empty environments for practical products
- Copying a reference image's exact composition, outfit, scene, or identifiable detail

## Practical Quality Test

Before output, ask:

- Can the user imagine the scene immediately?
- Can an AI platform generate it without guessing the missing context?
- Does the product belong in the scene?
- Does the action prove the scene is alive?
- Does the environment feel used, inhabited, or professionally specific?
- Is the prompt specific without being bloated?
- Would this still work after `video-director` adds shot size, camera movement, and transition separately?
